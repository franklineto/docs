01C1 Core

Formal Inversion

Fronting negative adverbials and conditionals for emphasis

Structure & Triggers

Negative/restrictive adverbial + auxiliary + subject + main verb
  • Never, Rarely, Seldom, Hardly, Scarcely, No sooner: invert after these.
  • Not only… but also, Not until, Only when, Only then: trigger inversion.
  • Conditional inversion: drop "if" → Had I known / Should you need / Were it not for
  • So/Such… that: fronted for emphasis → So great was his fear…

Examples

Standard
I had never seen such a sight.
Inverted
Never had I seen such a sight.
Standard
If I had known, I would have helped.
Inverted
Had I known, I would have helped.
Standard
Not only did she win, but she also broke the record.
Note
After "not only", auxiliary precedes subject in both clauses.

02C1 Core

The Subjunctive Mood

Formal mandative, formulaic and were-subjunctive

Three Types

  • Mandative (formal): after verbs of demand/suggestion + that → base verb for all persons.
    It is essential that every student be present. / We insisted that he leave immediately.
  • Were-subjunctive: hypothetical conditions → If I were you… / Were it possible…
  • Formulaic: fixed expressions → God save the King. Be that as it may. Long live democracy. So be it.
Mandative verbs: demand, insist, recommend, suggest, require, propose, request, urge, move, ask

04C1–C2

Ellipsis & Substitution

Omitting and replacing elements for conciseness and cohesion

Key Concepts

  • Ellipsis: omitting recoverable words → "Can you drive?" "I can [drive]."
  • Auxiliary ellipsis: She works harder than he does [work hard].
  • So/Not substitution: "Will it rain?" "I hope so / I hope not."
  • Do so: substitutes for a full verb phrase → He resigned, and his deputy did so too.
  • One/ones: noun substitution → I prefer the red one to the blue ones.
Ellipsis removes; substitution replaces. Both avoid unnecessary repetition.

05C1–C2

Advanced Modal Structures

Perfect modals, semi-modals and nuanced deduction

Perfect Modal Reference

StructureMeaningExample
must have + ppCertain past deduction (positive)She must have left already.
can't/couldn't have + ppCertain past deduction (impossible)He can't have done it — he was abroad.
might/may/could have + ppUncertain past possibilityThey might have misunderstood.
should have + ppPast unfulfilled expectation / criticismYou should have called ahead.
needn't have + ppUnnecessary past action (but it happened)You needn't have worried.
would have + ppUnreal past resultI would have helped had I known.

Semi-modals at C1+

  • be bound to: certainty → This is bound to cause problems.
  • be supposed to: expectation/obligation → You were supposed to submit it yesterday.
  • be about to: imminent action → She was about to resign when the offer came.
  • had better: strong advice → You had better reconsider that decision.
  • would rather: preference → I would rather you didn't mention it.

07C1–C2

Mixed Conditionals

Combining time frames and advanced conditional structures

Mixed Conditional Types

TypeIf-clauseResult clauseMeaning
Past → PresentIf + past perfectwould + baseUnreal past cause → present consequence
If he had studied law, he would be a barrister now.
Present → PastIf + past simplewould have + ppUnreal present condition → unreal past result
If she were more organised, she would have met the deadline.

Alternatives to If

  • Unless = if not: Unless you act now, the opportunity will be lost.
  • Provided / Providing (that): Provided that all conditions are met…
  • As long as / So long as: I'll support you as long as you're honest.
  • Supposing / Suppose (that): Supposing he refuses, what then?
  • Even if: Even if I apologise, she won't forgive me.
  • But for + noun/gerund: But for her intervention, the deal would have collapsed.

Academic & Formal Writing

Hedging, stance markers, complex syntax and formal alternatives

Hedging Language

  • Modal hedges: may, might, could, would → This could suggest that…
  • Lexical hedges: appear, seem, tend to, be likely to → The results appear to indicate…
  • Approximators: approximately, roughly, around, in the region of
  • Attribution: It is argued that, According to X, As noted by…

Informal → Formal Register Shifts

look intoinvestigate / examine / scrutinise
go up / go downincrease / rise / decline / decrease
a lot ofa considerable number/amount of, numerous, substantial
